By the third circuit she noticed the child.

He sat beneath the timetable with a suitcase too small to hold clothes and too heavy to be empty.
When Mina asked if he was waiting for someone, he opened the suitcase just enough for her to hear
music, thin and metallic, as if a tiny parade were trapped inside. “It only plays when the train is late,”
he said. “So today it will be very loud.”

The shadow of the station roof moved an inch across the concrete while he spoke. Mina felt, absurdly,
that the shadow was listening. A warm draft passed over the platform, carrying the smell of oranges
and engine oil. Somewhere beyond the tracks, a dog barked in a rhythm that almost matched the
flicker of the departure board.

At 8:19 a train appeared without sound.

It did not come along the rails. It assembled itself from reflections: first in the station windows, then
in the puddles by the vending machine, then in the polished black shoes of the man with the yellow
scarf. Only when everyone had already seen it did the train become solid enough to cast a shadow.

No one screamed. No one even looked surprised. The child clicked his suitcase shut and stood.
“Platform four,” he said, though there were only three.

Mina turned and found a fourth platform where the bicycle rack should have been. It was narrow,
painted a color she could not name, and lined with passengers she somehow recognized from places
she had never visited. A woman in a green coat held a birdcage full of folded maps. An old man
carried a potted fern wrapped in newspaper. A teenager in school uniform was reading a book with
all the pages blank except one.

The man in the yellow scarf tipped his hat to Mina as if they had met before. “You dropped this,”
he said, handing her a ticket printed with no destination, only a time: 8:21, and the words careful
what you remember.

The train doors opened inward, though there was no room for them to do so. Inside, the carriage
looked much larger than the station outside. Lamps swung gently from a ceiling painted with clouds.
The seats were upholstered in a fabric that changed pattern whenever she blinked. The child had
already taken a window seat and was winding a key in the side of his suitcase.

Mina stepped aboard.

At once the platform seemed to move away from her, not the train from it. Graywater station folded
itself smaller and smaller, like a note being tucked into a pocket. Through the glass she saw the
platform clock jump backward to 8:14. The loudspeaker announced, in a voice now soft as breath,
that all delays were temporary except the important ones.

“Tickets, please,” said a conductor who had not been there a moment before.

He wore no uniform, only a dark blue coat with silver stitching at the cuffs. Instead of punching
holes in the tickets, he held each one briefly to the window, where the passing light crossed over it
and burned a different shape into the paper. When he reached Mina, he paused.

“This one is dangerous,” he said.

Mina almost laughed. “It’s only paper.”

“Most dangerous things are,” he answered, and moved on.

The train gathered speed without any sensation of motion. Outside, the landscape changed in
careful, orderly ways: a quarry became a lake, the lake became a field of white umbrellas, the
umbrellas became sheep, and the sheep became stones. Villages appeared and vanished between
telegraph poles. Once, for half a minute, the train ran alongside itself, window to window, and Mina
saw her own reflection looking back from the other carriage, older and sharply attentive, as if waiting
for her to make a mistake.

The child’s suitcase began to play.

This time the music was not metallic but orchestral, full of horns and distant bells. With each note
the blank book in the teenager’s hands filled with text, then emptied again before Mina could read
more than a line. The woman with the birdcage opened its little door and one folded map escaped,
circling the carriage like a moth before landing in Mina’s lap.

It was a map of Graywater station.

Not the station she knew, but one with extra staircases, a glass roof, and a tunnel marked
for use only during thunderstorms. In the lower corner someone had written in neat blue ink:
If you miss your stop, do not admit it.

Mina looked up to ask whose map it was, but the woman in green was asleep, her chin resting on
the birdcage. The old man’s fern had begun to bloom with tiny paper flowers. Across the aisle, the
man in the yellow scarf was staring out the window with an expression of such careful attention
that Mina turned to look too.

They were passing through a city built entirely of bridges.

No streets, no buildings, only bridges crossing over and under one another in impossible layers,
hung with lamps that glowed in the daylight. People walked along them carrying ladders, mirrors,
bundles of reeds. Far below, where a river should have been, clouds moved slowly between the
arches.

“Is that real?” Mina asked.

The conductor, who seemed to be standing nearby whenever anyone spoke, considered the question.
“Real enough,” he said. “The trouble begins when people ask compared to what.”

The child laughed as if he had heard this before. He leaned across the aisle and offered Mina a
wrapped sweet from his pocket. The wrapper was printed with tiny station names she did not
recognize. She chose one at random and unwrapped it. Inside was not candy but a brass key.

“For later,” the child said.

“Later when?”

He shrugged. “When before becomes after.”

The train lights dimmed. A murmur passed through the carriage, not fear exactly, but the sound
people make when they sense a rule has changed. Outside the bridges gave way to open country,
flat and silver under a sky the color of unpolished tin. In the distance stood a house with every
window lit. As the train approached, Mina saw that the house was moving beside them, keeping
pace across the fields without wheels or tracks.

No one else seemed to find this remarkable.

The conductor walked down the aisle collecting watches. Some passengers surrendered wristwatches,
others pocket watches, one woman a kitchen timer. He placed them all in a leather case and snapped
it shut. When he reached Mina, she spread her hands. “I don’t have one.”

“Everyone has one,” he said gently.

Then he touched two fingers to the inside of her wrist, as if taking her pulse, and nodded to himself
before moving on.

The train entered a tunnel so wide that daylight remained around them for several seconds after the
walls enclosed the windows. In that fading light Mina saw words written on the tunnel bricks, huge
white letters repeating at intervals:

STAY AWAKE UNTIL THE SECOND WHISTLE.

The first whistle came almost immediately, long and low.

Several passengers closed their eyes. The old man hugged his fern. The teenager pressed both palms
over the blank book. The child with the suitcase looked suddenly much younger, his face emptied of
mischief. Mina gripped the brass key in her pocket and counted her breaths.

In the darkness between the first and second whistle, someone sat down beside her.

She was certain the seat had been empty. Now a woman occupied it, wearing a raincoat still wet at
the shoulders. Her hair was pinned back with two silver clips shaped like fish. She smelled faintly of
smoke and peppermint.

“You shouldn’t have boarded from platform four,” the woman said.

“There wasn’t a choice.”

“There is always a choice. That’s what makes it inconvenient.”

Mina turned to answer, but the second whistle split the tunnel air, bright and sharp. At once the
carriage lamps flared back to life. The woman beside her was gone, leaving only a damp crescent on
the seat.

The train emerged into morning again.

Not the same morning. This one was clearer, colder. Frost silvered the edges of the fields. A line of
black birds rose from a hedgerow and arranged themselves briefly into the shape of a word Mina
could almost read before they scattered.

The conductor opened the leather case and returned the watches. Some passengers smiled in relief.
Others looked disappointed, as if they had hoped to be given a different time than the one they’d
brought.

The man in the yellow scarf stood and took down a suitcase from the rack. “Next stop,” he said to
no one in particular.

Mina looked out the window and saw no station ahead, only a stand of leafless trees and, beyond
them, a frozen pond. Yet the train was slowing. The child snapped shut his little case of music and
slid from his seat.

“Is this where we get off?” Mina asked.

“If you like,” he said.

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the only kind we have.”

The train stopped beside the trees. The doors opened onto snow that bore no footprints. One by one
the passengers stepped down and walked between the trunks, vanishing almost immediately into the
white light beyond. The woman with the birdcage went first. Then the teenager with the blank book.
Then the old man carrying his fern like a lantern.

Mina remained seated until only she, the child, and the conductor were left.

“You can stay on,” said the conductor. “But the farther you go, the more accurate things become.”

“That sounds good.”

“It rarely is.”

The child was already standing in the doorway, his yellowish suitcase hanging from one hand. He
looked back at Mina with an expression she could not place—pity, perhaps, or warning, or simple
impatience.

In her pocket, the brass key had grown warm.

Mina rose and stepped down into the snow.

The cold struck cleanly, with none of the damp hesitation of city winter. The train behind her gave
a soft shudder, as if relieved. She turned to ask the conductor something—what, she did not know—
but the doors had already closed. Through the glass he lifted two fingers in a gesture that might have
been farewell or caution.

Then the train moved off without sound and was gone among the trees after only a few car lengths,
as though the forest had swallowed it whole.

Ahead, the other passengers were nowhere to be seen.

Only a narrow path crossed the snow toward the frozen pond. At its edge stood the moving house
she had seen from the train, now still and ordinary except for the fact that it had no chimney and
too many front steps. Smoke rose anyway from somewhere near the eaves.

Mina approached carefully. The brass key in her pocket pulsed once, like a second heartbeat.

On the front door hung a small enamel sign:

RING ONLY IF YOU HAVE FORGOTTEN SOMETHING.

Beneath it was no bell, only a keyhole.

Mina took out the key. It fit perfectly.

When she turned it, the pond behind her made a sound like a page being torn from a book. The door
opened inward on a hall lit by station lamps. Somewhere deeper in the house, a loudspeaker crackled,
and a voice she recognized as her own said, very clearly:

Now arriving. Please do not leave anything behind.
