In Chemistry, the different phases of matter are:

1. **Solid**: Characterized by a fixed shape and volume. The particles are closely packed in a regular arrangement and have minimal movement. Examples include ice, metals, and wood.

2. **Liquid**: Has a fixed volume but takes the shape of its container. The particles are close together but can move past one another, allowing the liquid to flow. Examples include water, oil, and mercury.

3. **Gas**: No fixed shape or volume; it expands to fill the container. The particles are far apart and move freely at high speeds. Examples include oxygen, nitrogen, and carbon dioxide.

4. **Plasma**: A high-energy state of matter where gas particles are ionized, resulting in a mixture of ions and free electrons. It is often found in stars, including the sun, and in certain types of lighting.

5. **Bose-Einstein Condensate**: At extremely low temperatures, certain atoms can occupy the same quantum state, behaving as a single entity. This phase is used in advanced physics research.

Each phase has distinct properties and behaviors due to the arrangement and movement of its particles.
