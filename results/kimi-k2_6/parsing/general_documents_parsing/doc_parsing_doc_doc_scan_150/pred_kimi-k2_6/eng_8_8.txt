\begin{center}
\textit{A proverbial Phrase.}
\end{center}

Dea dombvidla vöga öa vida vöga pesa (\textit{or passion}) vöga. Given with following explanation: \textit{Easter-Sunday weather will last all the week.} More correct probably: \textit{the weather of Passion-week will be the weather of Easter-week.}

The Conningsburgh phrase (acc. to Low): ``Myrk in e Liora, Luce in e Liunga, Tim in e Guest in e geungna'' (It's dark in the Chimney, but it's light thro' the Heath, It's still time for the stranger to be gone).

In Yell (in ``de Herra'') this phrase is still preserved in the following form:

\textit{Marka löra, lestra lngä, tämra göstra göngara [myrk er í ljöra, ljöst er í lyngi, tími er at gest(inn) gengi].} With regard to the form göngara, cf. Per. gongur.

\begin{center}
Our Lord's Prayer\footnote{A man still living in Unst was formerly able, according to his own and other's statements, to recite Our Lord's Prayer in Norn, or at any rate, the greater part of it. Some time ago, however, he had an illness which gradually enfeebled his memory, and when I looked him up, he could only remember the following corrupted fragments: --- \textit{ha tagat wa'rið name} (hallowed be Thy name) --- \textit{site himblins} (as in Heaven).} from Foula (reported by Low in his ``Tour thro' Orkney and Shetland'', reprinted by Barry and in ``Annaler for nordisk oldkyndighed'', 1860):
\end{center}

Fy vor o er i Chimeri. Halaght vara nam det. La Konungdum din cumma. La vill din vera guerde i vrilðin sinda eri Chimeri. Gav vus dagh i dagligt brau. Forgive sindor wusa (wara?) sin vi forgiva gem ao sinda gainst wus. Lia vus ek o vera tempa, but de-livra wus fro adlu idlu, for do i ir Konungdum, u puri, u glori. Amen.

\begin{center}
\textit{An Incantation.}
\end{center}

This is found in the tale: ``Da Tief i' de Neean'' (Shetland Times 1879).

The tale itself, as well as the language used in it, doubtless originates from Al. (Westa).

The formula, which is mainly in Scoto-English, has Norn words sprinkled throughout, some of which show old grammatical endings, that have lost their significance. It runs as follows:

\begin{verse}
Da stuhl es scarp an fien,\\
Da sacheles emer a snean,\\
Da vird es sicör en pura,\\
A glimer i' mirk-ss-dim hura,\\
La stuhl an vird ay gyrða\\
An prof er an skyla, an svirða.
\end{verse}
