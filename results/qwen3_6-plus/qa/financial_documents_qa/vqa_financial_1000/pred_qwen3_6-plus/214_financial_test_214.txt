This table, labeled **Table 1**, presents data on **class performance recorded after taking a course on Food and Nutrition**. Specifically, it shows the **percentage of students in the 1975 and 1976 classes who agreed with the course teachings**, broken down by different categories of questions that were part of a 50-question test.

The table is organized as follows:

- **Rows**: Different characterizations of the questions raised in the test:
  1. 17 questions relating to established economic or scientific facts.
  2. 17 questions relating to confirmed scientific findings but still rejected by consumer activists.
  3. 16 questions pitting professional scientists’ opinions against charges from consumer activists.
  4. The entire test (50 questions total).

- **Columns**: Percentage agreement with course teachings for two cohorts:
  - 1975 Class
  - 1976 Class

### Key Observations:
- In both years, agreement was highest for questions based on “established economic or scientific facts” (88% in 1975, 86% in 1976).
- Agreement was lowest for questions involving “confirmed scientific findings rejected by consumer activists” (82% in 1975, 78% in 1976), suggesting some resistance to accepting science when it contradicts activist views.
- Overall agreement across all 50 questions was 84% in 1975 and 81% in 1976 — indicating high but slightly declining alignment with course teachings over time.

### Purpose:
The table appears to measure how well students internalized or accepted the course’s perspective — particularly in areas where scientific consensus might conflict with popular or activist beliefs. It may be used to evaluate the effectiveness of the course in shaping student understanding or attitudes toward food and nutrition science.
