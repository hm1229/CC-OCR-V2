\begin{center}
\textit{A proverbial Phrase.}
\end{center}

Dea dombvldia vöga dsa vidla vöga þesa (or passion) vöga.\\
Given with following explanation: \textit{Easter-Sunday weather will last all the week. More correct probably: the weather of Passion-week will be the weather of Easter-week.}

The Conningsburgh phrase facc. to Low): ``Myrk in e Liöra, Luce in e Liung, Tim in e Guest in e geungna'' (it's dark in the Chimney, but it's light thro' the Heath, it's still time for the stranger to be gone).

In Yell (in ``de Herra'') this phrase is still preserved in the following form:\\
Mörka iöra, lestra linga, tämra grestra göngera [myrkt er i fjöra, ljöst er i lyngi, timi er at gestrinn) gengr]. With regard to the form göngera, cf. Fær. gongur.

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\textit{Our Lord's Prayer}\textsuperscript{1} from Foula (reported by Low in his ``Tour thro' Orkney and Shetland'', reprinted by Barry and in ``Annaler for nordisk oldkyndighed'' 1860):
\end{center}

Fy vor o er i Chimeri. Halaght vara nam det. La Konungdum din cimuma. La vill din vera guerde i vrildin sinda er i Chimeri. Gav vus dagh u dagloght brau. Forgive sinder wasa (vara?) sind vi foröfiva gem ao sinda ganst vus. Lia wus eke o vara tempa, but de-livra wus fro adlu idlu, for do i ir Konungdum, u puri, u glori. Amen.

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\textit{An Incantation.}
\end{center}

This is found in the tale: ``Da Tief i' de Neean'' (Shetland Times 1879).

The tale itself, as well as the language used in it, doubtless originates from AI. (Wests.).

The formula, which is mainly in Scoto-English, has Norn words sprinkled throughout, some of which show old grammatical endings, that have lost their significance. It runs as follows:

\begin{center}
Da stuhl es scarp an fien,\\
Da sacheles emer a snean,\\
Da vird es söker en pua,\\
A glimer i' mirk-as-dim hura,\\
La stuhl an vird ay gyrda\\
An prof er an skyla, an svärda.
\end{center}

\bigskip

\noindent
\textsuperscript{1}~I am still living in Unst was formerly able, according to his own and other's statements, to recite Our Lord's Prayer in Norn, at any rate, the greater part of it. Some time ago, however, he had an illness which gradually enfeebled his memory, and when I looked him up, he could only remember the following corrupted fragments: \textit{I halagad wa'ts name} (hallowed be Thy name) --- \textit{sae kembla} (as in heaven).
