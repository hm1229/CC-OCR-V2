Yugoslavia pardoned **Lazlo Toth**, a Thornton, Colorado resident and naturalized American citizen, who had been jailed for “industrial espionage.”

### Key Details:
- **Crime**: Accused of taking photographs of a beet-sugar plant in Vrbas (a suburb of Belgrade) while vacationing in Yugoslavia. He was director of research and development at Great Western Sugar Co. in Loveland, Colo.
- **Sentence**: Seven years in prison after a closed-door trial.
- **Pardon Announcement**: Made by the Yugoslav collective presidency (including President Tito) to the U.S. Embassy in Belgrade on Thursday, July 15, 1976 — one day before the Denver Post article was published (July 16, 1976).
- **Release Date**: Unknown at time of reporting. U.S. officials were pressing for a specific date.
- **Family Reaction**: His wife, Zora Toth, expressed delight but remained cautious until he was physically back in the U.S.

The pardon came after nearly a year of diplomatic efforts, including involvement from Rep. Jim Johnson’s office and rumors of clemency tied to President Tito’s birthday traditions.

> *“It is not only talk anymore. He will be coming.”* — Mrs. Zora Toth

**Source**: *The Denver Post*, Friday, July 16, 1976 — Article titled:  
**“RELEASE DATE UNKNOWN — Yugoslavia Pardons Thornton Man Jailed for Espionage”**  
By Pat Raybon, Denver Post Staff Writer.

*(Note: The headline says “Thornton Man,” referring to Lazlo Toth’s residence in Thornton, Colorado.)*
