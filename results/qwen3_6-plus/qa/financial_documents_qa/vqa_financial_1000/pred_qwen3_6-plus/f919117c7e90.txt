This is a **Discipline Review Letter** issued by the **U.S. Department of Health & Human Services (HHS)**, specifically from the **Food and Drug Administration (FDA)** under the **Public Health Service**.

### Key Details:

- **Issuing Agency**: FDA, Rockville, MD
- **Recipient**: Takeda Global Research & Development Center, Inc. — specifically addressed to Mary Jo Pritza, MPH, PharmD, Manager, Regulatory Affairs
- **Subject**: Review of supplemental New Drug Application (NDA 21-073/S-026) for **Actos® (pioglitazone HCl)** tablets (15 mg, 30 mg, 45 mg)
- **Date Received by Takeda**: February 19, 2007
- **Reference Dates**: 
  - Original submission: January 24, 2006
  - Subsequent submissions: June 1, August 22 & 31, October 10, November 14, December 15, 2006
  - FDA’s proposed labeling revisions sent via email: February 7, 2007

---

### Purpose of the Letter:

The FDA is communicating the results of its review of Takeda’s response to proposed labeling changes for Actos®. Specifically, it addresses:

> **Movement of information regarding the PROactive trial outcome**  
> — from the *Clinical Studies* section → to the *Adverse Reactions* section  
> — and inclusion of data **only from the primary endpoint**

---

### Rationale Provided by FDA:

1. **The PROactive trial was a failed trial** — statistical significance for the **primary endpoint was not met**, so secondary endpoints cannot be considered per standard regulatory/statistical principles.

2. Even if secondary endpoints were statistically legitimate:
   - The proposed “main” secondary endpoint was added to the analysis plan **long after the trial ended**.
   - The timing (months between study end and endpoint addition, then days before unblinding) makes it appear like a **post hoc analysis**, not a prospectively defined one — which undermines its validity in regulatory decision-making.

### Classification:

While titled “Discipline Review Letter,” this is effectively an **FDA regulatory correspondence** providing feedback on labeling proposals for a drug application. It reflects the FDA’s role in ensuring that drug labeling accurately reflects clinical evidence and adheres to statistical and regulatory standards.

It may also serve as part of the official record for potential enforcement or compliance actions — hence the term “discipline” — though no penalty is imposed here; rather, it’s a scientific/regulatory justification for rejecting certain labeling claims.
