\textsc{sc. I.]} \textbf{PRINCE OF DENMARK} \hfill 101  

And makes us rather bear those ills we have, \\  
Than fly to others that we know not of? \\  
Thus conscience does make cowards of us all, \\  
And thus the native hue of resolution \\  
Is sicklied o’er with the pale cast of thought, \footnotemark{85} \\  
And enterprises of great pitch and moment \\  
With this regard their currents turn awry \\  
And lose the name of action. \textit{Soft you now!} \\  
The fair Ophelia? \textit{Nymph, in thy orisons} \\  
Be all my sins remember’d. \\  
\textit{Oph.} \\  
How does your honour for this many a day? \footnotemark{90}  


\footnotetext{83. of us all] Q I, F; omitted Q. away F.} \\  
\footnotetext{86. pitch] Q, pitch F.} \\  
\footnotetext{87. awry] Q, away F.} \\  
\footnotetext{89. Ophelia] F, Ophelia, Q, Ophelia! Hanmer.} \\  
\footnotetext{85. thought] often used of anxious or melancholy thought, as in *Julius Caesar*, U. i. 187: ``take thought and die for Caesar.'' See iv. v. 187.} \\  
\footnotetext{86. pitch] height, as in *King Richard III*. iii. viii. 188; used of a falcon’s soaring, 1 *King Henry VI*. i. ii. iv. The Folio *pitch* is preferred by many editors, and appears in late Quartos from 1676 onwards.} \\  
\footnotetext{88. action] With the thought of action this soliloquy opens and closes. The train of ideas is as follows: — Active resistance to evil or passive fortitude—which is more worthy of me? To end troubles—perhaps by one’s own death? Well, the sleep of death will be most welcome; but what if there be terrible dreams? The fear of the hereafter is universal, else men would not endure the ills of life; and thus it is that, perplexed by calculating consequences, we drop away from heroic action.
