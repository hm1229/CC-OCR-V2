\subsection*{A proverbial Phrase.}

Dæ dombvíla vǫga dæ vilda vǫga pessa (or passion) vǫga.
Given with following explanation: Easter-Sunday weather will last
all the week. More correct probably: the weather of Passion-week
will be the weather of Easter-week.

The Coningsburgh phrase (acc. to Low): ``Myrk ín e Lióra, Luce
ín e Liunga, Tim ín e Quest ín e geungna'' (It's dark in the Chim-
ney, but it's light thro' the Heath, it's still time for the stranger to
be gone.)

In Yell (in ``de Herrea'') this phrase is still preserved in the fol-
lowing form:
Márka lóra, lestra lúnga, támra geystra gongara [myrk't er í lóra,
ljoest er í lúnga, tími er, at geistr(inn) gengr]. With regard to the form
gongara, cf. Far. gongur.

\subsection*{An Incantation.}

This is found in the tale: ``Da Tief í' de Neean'' (Shetland
Times 1879).

The tale itself, as well as the language used in it, doubtless or-
iginates from Ai. (West.)

The formula, which is mainly in Scoto-English, has Norn words
sprinkled throughout, some of which show old grammatical endings,
that have lost their significance. It runs as follows:

Da stuhl es scarc an fiern,
Da saeches emer a sdeen,
Da vird es sicer en púra,
A glimer í' mirk-as-dim húra,
La stuhl an vird ay gydda
An prof er an skyla, an svírdra.
