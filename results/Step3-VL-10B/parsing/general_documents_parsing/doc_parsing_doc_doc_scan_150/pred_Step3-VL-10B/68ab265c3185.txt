\textbf{History of Computer}

The computer began in 19th century by Charles Babbage English mathematics professor. He designed the Analytical Engine and became to the basic framework of the computers until nowadays.  

To be able to classified the computer for three generations. Each generation lasted for a certain period of time, and each gave us either a new and improved computer or an improvement to the existing computer.  


\textbf{First generation: 1937 – 1946;} In 1937, a computer was built by Dr. John V. Atanasoff and Clifford Berry. It is the first electronic digital computer, called the Atanasoff–Berry Computer (ABC). Afterwards, an electronic computer named Colossus was built for military use. Other developments continued until 1946, when the first general-purpose digital computer—the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer (ENIAC)—was built. ENIAC was massive: weighing about 30 tons and using 18,000 vacuum tubes for processing. When powered on, lights dimmed in sections of Philadelphia. Computers of this generation could only perform single tasks and had no operating system.

\textbf{Second generation: 1947 – 1962;} Computers used transistors (more reliable than vacuum tubes). This generation introduced computers to the public commercially—for example, the Universal Automatic Computer (UNIVAC 1). In 1953, IBM’s 650 and 700 series marked the industry. Over 100 programming languages were developed, computers had memory and operating systems, and storage media (tape, disk) plus printers for output became standard.

\textbf{Third generation: 1963 – present;} The invention of integrated circuits enabled this generation. Computers became smaller, more powerful, and reliable, running multiple programs simultaneously. In 1980, Microsoft Disk Operating System (MS-DOS) launched; in 1981, IBM introduced the personal computer (PC) for home/office use. Three years later, Apple released the Macintosh (icon-driven interface), and the 1990s brought Windows operating system.
