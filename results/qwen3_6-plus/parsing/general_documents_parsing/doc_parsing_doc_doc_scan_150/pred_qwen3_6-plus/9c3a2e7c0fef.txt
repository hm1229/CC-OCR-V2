\subsection*{A proverbial Phrase.}

Dea dombvida vöga dsa vidla vöga pësa (or passion) vöga. Given with following explanation: \textit{Easter-Sunday weather will last all the week}. More correct probably: the weather of Passion-week will be the weather of Easter-week.

The Conningsburgh phrase (acc. to Low): “Myrk in e Liora, Luce in e Liunga, Tim in e Guest in e geungna” (It’s dark in the Chimney, but it’s light thro’ the Heath, it’s still time for the stranger to be gone).

In Yell (in “de Herra”) this phrase is still preserved in the following form:

\subsection*{An Incantation.}

This is found in the tale: “Da Tjef i’ de Neean” (Shetland Times 1879).

The tale itself, as well as the language used in it, doubtless originates from Ai. (Wests.).

The formula, which is mainly in Scots-English, has Norn words sprinkled throughout, some of which show old grammatical endings, that have lost their significance. It runs as follows:

\begin{quote}
    Da stuhl es scarp an fien, \\
    Da sacheles emer a snean, \\
    Da vird es sicer en pura, \\
    A glimer i’ mirk-as-dim hura, \\
    La stuhl an vird ay gyrda \\
    An prof er an skyla, an svirda.
\end{quote}

\footnotetext[1]{A man still living in Unst was formerly able, according to his own and other’s statements, to recite Our Lord’s Prayer in Norn, or at any rate, the greater part of it.
