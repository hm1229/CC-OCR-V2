\noindent\textsc{sc. i.]} \hfill \textbf{PRINCE OF DENMARK} \hfill \textbf{101}

\bigskip

\begin{verse}
And makes us rather bear those ills we have \\
Than fly to others that we know not of? \\
Thus conscience does make cowards of us all, \\
And thus the native hue of resolution \\
Is sicklied o'er with the pale cast of thought, \hfill 85 \\
And enterprises of great pitch and moment \\
With this regard their currents turn awry \\
And lose the name of action. \quad Soft you now! \\
The fair Ophelia? \quad Nymph, in thy orisons \\
Be all my sins remember'd.
\end{verse}

\noindent\textit{Oph.} \hfill Good my lord, \hfill 90

\begin{verse}
How does your honour for this many a day?
\end{verse}

\noindent 83. \textit{of us all}] Q \textsc{i}, F; omitted Q. \quad 86. \textit{pitch}] Q, \textit{pith} F. \quad 87. \textit{awry}] Q, \textit{away} F. \quad 89. \textit{Ophelia?}] F, \textit{Ophelia}, Q, \textit{Ophelia!} Hanmer.

\bigskip

\begin{multicols}{2}
\noindent\small
85. \textit{thought}] often used of anxious
or melancholy thought, as in \textit{Julius
C{\ae}sar}, \textsc{ii}. i. 187: ``take thought and
die for C{\ae}sar.'' See \textsc{iv}. v. 187.

86. \textit{pitch}] height, as in \textit{King
Richard III}. \textsc{iii}. vii. 188; used of
a falcon's soaring, \textit{1 King Henry VI}.
\textsc{ii}. iv. 11. The Folio \textit{pith} is preferred
by many editors, and appears in late
Quartos from 1676 onwards.

88. \textit{action}] With the thought of
action this soliloquy opens and closes.
The train of ideas is as follows:---
Active resistance to evil or passive
fortitude---which is more worthy of
me? To end troubles---perhaps by
one's own death? Well, the sleep
of death will be most welcome; but
what if there be terrible dreams?
The fear of the hereafter is universal,
else men would not endure the ills of
life; and thus it is that, perplexed by
calculating consequences, we drop
away from heroic action. Parallels,
as possible sources for parts of this
soliloquy, have been pointed out in
Catullus (no traveller returns), Cardan
(death a sleep), Seneca (no traveller
returns, and fear of futurity), Mon-

\columnbreak

\noindent taigne (sea of troubles, death a desir-
able ``consummation,'' conscience
makes cowards), Cornelius Agrippa
(country of the dead irremeable),
Marlowe's \textit{Edward II}. (Mortimer
goes as a traveller to discover
countries yet unknown). It seems
probable, as Professor Skeat notices,
that there are reminiscences here of
the translation ascribed to Chaucer of
\textit{The Romaunt of the Rose}, lines 5637--
5696; the word \textit{fardels} is perhaps
one of the echoes from this passage.
It is worth noting that Mr. G.
Macdonald eliminates the thought of $\cdot$
suicide from the soliloquy, supposing
that the bare bodkin is imagined as
directed against an enemy. Suicide,
indeed, is not the theme of the soli-
loquy, but it incidentally enters into
it. ``Clelia'' in his \textit{God in Shake-
speare} construes the opening sentence
thus: ``Whether 'tis nobler to bear
evil or to resist it the question is To
be, or not to be, \textit{i.e.} Is there a life
after death?'' The note of interroga-
tion after ``end them,'' line 60, was
first introduced by Pope.
