\begin{center}
\textit{A proverbial Phrase.}
\end{center}

D\ae a dombvidla v\~oga d\ae a vidla v\~oga p\ae sa (\textit{or} \textit{passion}) v\~oga.
Given with following explanation: \textit{Easter-Sunday weather will last
all the week.} More correct probably: \textit{the weather of Passion-week
will be the weather of Easter-week.}

The Conningsburgh phrase (acc.\ to Low): ``Myrk in e L\overline{\i}ora, Luce
in e Liunga, Tim in e Guest in e geungna'' (It's dark in the Chimney, but it's light thro' the Heath, it's still time for the stranger to
be gone).

In Yell (in ``de Herra'') this phrase is still preserved in the following form:

Ma\textquotesingle rka l\overline{\i}ora, lestra lnga, t\~amra g\ae stra g\o ngara [myrkt er \'{i} lj\'{o}ra,
lj\'{o}st er \'{i} lyngi, timi er, at gestr(inn) gengr]. With regard to the form
g\o ngara, cf.\ F\ae r.\ g\"{o}ngur.

\bigskip

Our Lord's Prayer\textsuperscript{1} from Foula (reported by Low in his ``Tour
thro' Orkney and Shetland'', reprinted by Barry and in ``Annaler for
nordisk oldkyndighed'', 1860):

Fy vor o er i Chimeri. Halaght vara nam det. La Konungdum
din cumma. La vill din vera guerde i vrildin sinda eri Chimeri. Gav
vus daglu u dagloght brau. Forgive sindor wasa (wara?) sin vi forgiva gem ao sinda gainst wus. Lia wus eke o vera tempa, but delivra wus fro adlu idlu, for do i ir Konungdum, u puri, u glori. Amen.

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\textit{An Incantation.}
\end{center}

This is found in the tale: ``Da Tief i' de Neean'' (Shetland
Times 1879).

The tale itself, as well as the language used in it, doubtless originates from Al.\ (Wests.).

The formula, which is mainly in Scoto-English, has Norn words
sprinkled throughout, some of which show old grammatical endings,
that have lost their significance. It runs as follows:

\begin{verse}
Da stuhl es scarp an fien,\\
Da sacheles emer a snean,\\
Da vird es sicer en pura,\\
A glimer i' mirk-as-din hura,\\
La stuhl an vird ay gyrda\\
An prof er an skyla, an svirda.
\end{verse}

\bigskip

\footnotesize
\textsuperscript{1} A man still living in Unst was formerly able, according to his own and other's
statements, to recite Our Lord's Prayer in Norn, or at any rate, the greater part of
it. Some time ago, however, he had an illness which gradually enfeebled his memory, and when I looked him up, he could only remember the following corrupted
fragments: --- \textit{halagat wa'rta name (hallowed be Thy name)} --- \textit{sae
himblins (as in Heaven).}
