\subsection*{A proverbial Phrase.}

Dēa dombvīda võga dśa vidā võga (pōrass) võga. With given following explanation: Easter-Sunday weather will last all the week. More correctly probable: the weather of Passion-week.  

The Congresbyng passage (acc. to Low): “My ūr in Lēca, in Lēca, Tim in a Guest in e geungna” (It’s dark in the Chimney, but it’s light th’ro the Heath, It’s still time for the stranger to be gone).  

In Yell (in “de Hera”) this phrase is still preserved in the following form: Mārka lōra, lessen līga, tāmā gēstra gōngara [myrkt e lōjā], lōjst e līgyn, tim e, at gēst(in) gēngra. With regard to the form gōngara, cf. Fāer. gōngura.

\subsection*{An Incantation.}

This is found in the tale: “Da Tief i’ de Neenan” (Sealt Times 1879). The tale itself, as well as the language used in it, doubtless originates from A. (Wests). The formula, which is mainly in Scoto-English, has Norm words sprinkled throughout, some of which show old grammatical endings, that have lost their significance. It runs as follows:  

\begin{quote}
Da stuhl es scarp an fīen, \\
Da sacheles emer a slean, \\
A dyr vīs e cīsper un pura, \\
A glīmer i’ mirk–ad–dim hura, \\
La staul in vyr ad yāgdy, \\
An prof e anr skyla, an svīrda.
\end{quote}
