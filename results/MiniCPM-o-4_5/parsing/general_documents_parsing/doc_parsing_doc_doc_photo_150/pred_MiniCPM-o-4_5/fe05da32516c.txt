\text{Problem 1}

\text{}
\text{In the lecture "syntax" I develop a toy language Little English. It has the following lexicon (each expression belongs to exactly one grammatical category: names, predicates, or connectives).}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{Name: Kate, Billy}
\text{}
\text{Predicates: smokes, sleeps}
\text{}
\text{Connectives: and, or}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{Furthermore I define well-formed formula in Little English as follows:}
\text{}
\text{1. If $\Phi$ is a name, and $\Psi$ is a predicate, then "$\Phi \Psi$" is a well-formed formula (wff).}
\text{}
\text{2. If $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ are well-formed formulas, and $\Omega$ is a connective, then "$\Phi \Omega \Psi$" is a wff.}
\text{}
\text{3. Nothing else is a wff.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{I pointed out that the expression Kate sleeps and smokes is not a wff.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{This is counterintuitive. Kate sleeps and smokes is a perfectly grammatical sentence of English. Fortunately, there is an easy way to fix this. We can add the following condition to the definition of wff.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{(C) If $\Phi$ is a well-formed formula, $\Psi$ is a predicate, and $\Omega$ is a connective, then "$\Phi \Omega \Psi$" is a wff.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{Which yields the following revised definition of wff:}
\text{}
\text{1. If $\Phi$ is a name, and $\Psi$ is a predicate, then "$\Phi \Psi$" is a well-formed formula (wff).}
\text{}
\text{2. If $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ are well-formed formulas, and $\Omega$ is a connective, then "$\Phi \Omega \Psi$" is a wff.}
\text{}
\text{3. If $\Phi$ is a well-formed formula, $\Psi$ is a predicate, and $\Omega$ is a connective, then "$\Phi \Omega \Psi$" is a wff.}
\text{}
\text{4. Nothing else is a wff.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{a) Using the revised definition, show that Kate sleeps and smokes is a wff.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{b) Using the revised definition, show that Kate smokes or Bill sleeps and smokes is a wff.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{c) Explain why Bill or Kate sleeps is not a wff under the revised definition.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{d) Further revise the revised definition, so that Bill or Kate sleeps counts as a wff. You have to add it to a new condition, like I did with (c) above.}
\text{}
\text{}
\text{e) Using your new definition, show that Bill or Kate sleeps is a wff.}
\text{}
