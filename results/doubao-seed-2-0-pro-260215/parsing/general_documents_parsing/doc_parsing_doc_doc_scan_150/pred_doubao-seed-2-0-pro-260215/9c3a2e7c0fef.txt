\subsection*{A proverbial Phrase.}

\noindent D\textit{ea} dombv\i{}dia v\={o}ga d\textit{ea} v\i{}dla v\={o}ga p\={e}sa \textit{(or passion)} v\={o}ga.
Given with following explanation: \textit{Easter-Sunday weather will last
all the week.} More correct probably: \textit{the weather of Passion-week
will be the weather of Easter-week.}

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent The Conningsburgh phrase (acc. to Low): ``Myrk in e Liora, Luce
in e Liunga, Tim in e Quest in e geungna'' (It's dark in the Chim-
ney, but it's light thro' the Heath, it's still time for the stranger to
be gone).

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent In Yell (in ``de Herra'') this phrase is still preserved in the fol-
lowing form:

\noindent M\textit{ə}'rka l\={o}ra, lestra l\i{}nga, t\=amra g\textit{e}stra g\o{}ng\textit{g}ara [myrkt er \'{\i} lj\'ora,
lj\'ost er \'{\i} lyngi, t\'imi er, at gestr(inn) gengr]. With regard to the form
g\o{}ng\textit{g}ara, cf. F\ae{}r. gongur.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent Our Lord's Prayer\textsuperscript{1} from Foula (reported by Low in his ``Tour
thro' Orkney and Shetland'', reprinted by Barry and in ``Annaler for
nordisk oldkyndighed'', 1860):

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent Fy vor o er i Chimeri. Halaght vara nam det. La Konungdum
din cumma. La vill din vera guerde i vrildin sinda eri Chimeri. Gav
vus dagh u dagloght brau. Forgive sindor wasa (wara?) sin vi for-
giva gem ao sinda gainst wus. Lia wus eke o vera tempa, but de-
livra wus fro adlu idlu, for do i ir Konungdum, u puri, u glori. Amen.

\vspace{1em}
\subsection*{An Incantation.}

\noindent This is found in the tale: ``Da Tief i' de Neean'' (Shetland
Times 1879).

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent The tale itself, as well as the language used in it, doubtless or-
iginates from Ai. (Wests.).

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent The formula, which is mainly in Scoto-English, has Norn words
sprinkled throughout, some of which show old grammatical endings,
that have lost their significance. It runs as follows:

\begin{verse}
Da stuhl es scarp an fien,\\
Da sacheles emer a snean,\\
Da vird es sicer en pura,\\
A glimer i' mirk-as-dim hura,\\
La stuhl an vird ay gyrda\\
An prof er an skyla, an svirda.
\end{verse}

\vspace{1em}
\footnotetext[1]{A man still living in Unst was formerly able, according to his own and other's
statements, to recite Our Lord's Prayer in Norn, or at any rate, the greater part of
it. Some time ago, however, he had an illness which gradually enfeebled his mem-
ory, and when I looked him up, he could only remember the following corrupted
fragments; —— \textit{halagəl wa'rtə name (hallowed be Thy name)} —— \textit{sæ
himblins (as in Heaven)}.}
