\subsection*{Formulas For a Line}
You can think of the slope-intercept form of an equation for a line, \( y = mx + b \), as a formula for the line given \( m \) and \( b \).

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{Example:} Write the formula for a line given two points \( (x_1, y_1) \) and \( (x_2, x_2) \).

\vspace{0.5em}
Use the idea that the slope of a line is the same between any two points.
$$\frac{y - y_1}{x - x_1} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

\noindent \begin{tabular}{l l}
Multiply each & \\
side by \( (x - x_1) \). &
\( \displaystyle y - y_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}(x - x_1) \quad \textit{Two-point formula} \)
\end{tabular}

\vspace{1em}
If you know two points of a line, you can write an equation for the line directly using this formula.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent \textbf{Use the two-point formula to write an equation for the line that passes through the given points.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \( (3, 1) \) and \( (2, -1) \)
    \item \( (-3, 1) \) and \( (6, 7) \)
    \item \( (-4, 4) \) and \( (0, 3) \)
    \item \( (-5, -3) \) and \( (7, -3) \)
    \item \( (10, 0) \) and \( (0, 9) \)
    \item \( (a, 0) \) and \( (0, b) \)
\end{enumerate}
